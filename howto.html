How To Bench Press

The rules, the setup, and the bar path — everything for a solid, valid press.

The Rules at the Bench Press

You must maintain 5 points of contact on the bench press, your glutes must stay on the bench, both shoulders must stay planted to the bench, your head must stay on the bench, and both your feet should stay planted to the floor. If your glutes come off the bench, a foot comes up, you do not touch your chest with the bar, your head comes off the bench, or you don’t lock out your elbows at the top the lift is considered not valid.

The Bar Path

The bar path is the path the bar travels during the lift. The ideal bar path consists of two parts: First, lay down so the bar is about eye level then reach up and try to pul the bar off of the pins or hooks of the bench press without losing your arch. Then lower the bar towards your lower chest until it touches. Second, and most importantly, press the bar back up and towards eyes in a reverse motion of what you did to let the weight descend.

Setting Up

The first thing to do when setting up for a bench press is to set a proper “arch” in your back. Why? Because, “A flat-backed press can cause your shoulders to roll forward, putting immense stress on the joint. Arching allows you to safely pin your shoulder blades (scapulae) back and down into the bench, providing a stable foundation and reducing the risk of impingement or injury.” -Squat University. Arching also reduces the range of motion for the lift allowing you to lift heavier. An arch is done by pulling shoulders down and holding them tightly while raising chest to the ceiling. It will take some practice, but a good arch is the foundation of a bench press.

Why Does Bar Path Matter?

Pressing upwards and backwards allows more use of pectoral muscles and triceps and less stress on the shoulders. If you touch your chest and press the weight directly up or up and towards your hips the lift will most likely be much more strenuous. A good way to check your bar path is record from the side while you’re lifting and imagine the end of the bar drawing a line.
